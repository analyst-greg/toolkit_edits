Macro-trends in Charitable Giving

  • Small-dollar donor participation is in steep decline (economy, wealth concentration, etc)
  • Similarly, total number of donors is going down (more high-dollar donors everyone is chasing after)
  • Institutional trust frequently shredded by scandal
  • Your average donor who isn’t a billionaire or multi-millionaire may be more compelled by one-off projects, or local programs, or things that they think have social impact that aren’t explicitly philanthropy
  • Elon Musk quote that is very revealing: “My businesses are my philanthropy”, as in, contributing to the overall economic output of the country via enterprise translates to downstream benefits for the average person more than giving them money via philanthropic vehicles. Any reactions to that?

Fundraising as a career

Fundraising career paths

  • Many people end up doing at least some fundraising, if they work in the nonprofit sector
  • Generally every fundraising equivalent has a for-profit counterpart or close analogue (IT, sales, marketing, accounting, etc)
  • Like in business, the highest paid professionals tend to be those with a portfolio (in this case, of donors instead of accounts), who bring in revenue, and then the C-suite or upper admin
  • Plenty of other operational roles, which deal with varying levels of chaos
  • In my role, I make $60k a year, started off in the mid-40s, full-time, with benefits
  • Many pros and many cons, like with any job, one of the pros for me is that I get to work for a University, and since I find education to be a fundamental social good, some of my personal values are represented in my job
  • Unfortunately, networking is the best way to improve your career (when I applied for my job at the U, they already knew me from a conference we attended together as colleagues, and from being in the same professional organization), and for those of us who are sometimes miserable in social situations, I promise there is a way to make it work for you, usually by focusing on those things you are passionate about and seeking out people’s advice or expertise

How it looks at UVU

  • Division is called “Institutional Advancement”, UVU does a couple odd things with how their org chart has shaken out in its details, but generally you’ll see these roles / teams in other higher ed fundraising teams, and many similarities with Hospital fundraising teams, Nonprofits are highly varied, so they may diverge, but the bigger ones also tend to have similar org charts
  • VP is in the University President’s Cabinet, one of the lead fundraisers, alongside the President
  • AVP of Development is the leader of the frontline fundraisers, and is a fundraiser themselves, they lead the development team (at UVU they are called Directors of Development, but job titles vary, like Major Gift Officer), these folks tend to be more extroverted, often working with specific colleges especially alongside the Deans to fund programs, scholarships, etc…and you have several related teams like Annual Giving, Planned Giving, Alumni Relations (if there wasn’t so much event planning, I would personally love to work in this world more), and Prospect Dev (sometimes Prospect Dev is on the data team instead, people sometimes don’t know where it fits)
  • Senior Director of Foundation Finance and Compliance, runs the endowment, has to host auditors when they drop by (a stressful day), leads the accountants, tries to keep everybody realistic
  • AVP of Strategic Engagement, leads the Executive Events team, and the Marketing & Communications (Marcom is common jargon there) team: this is where you have your web designers, your graphic designers, content creators, and event planners.
  • All these leaders often have executive assistants

How it looks in the for-profit world

  • Many companies have foundations or social impact missions that require operational staff
  • You will make more many in the for-profit world (e.g. an account manager at a Fortune 500 company instead of a development officer at a college)

Your values vs. your job

  • In nonprofit and for-profit sectors, your values may drive some of your career moves, and you have to draw your own lines in the sand (e.g. would you work for a Political Action Committee? What kind? Religious orgs are the biggest recipents of charitable donations, many of them need fundraising staff, but what if the one you are working for has a scandal?)
  • A single year can bring massive changes, especially if you don’t work for an institution that has an endowment, those changes can test you
  • If, like me when I graduated, you just want to land any job that can pay the rent and give you healthcare, it can be difficult to take your values into account if they feel like roadblocks
