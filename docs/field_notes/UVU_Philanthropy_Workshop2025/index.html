Philanthropy at the Student Scale

  • Less than, say, $1000 or less over ~5 years
  • The Brooklyn Org example, micro DAF 2
  • Giving Days at the U example, some mini drives led by students + matching programs
  • Matching gifts and Microsoft rewards 3

  • Even more than making a private, small donation - a program/project/non-profit may appreciate you organizing a small drive or campaign on their behalf. Some jobs, for example, will match your donation to a list of approved non-profits, which makes your money stretch farther and have more impact. Your coworkers may not know about the matching program, so organizing a small office drive to take advantage of the matching (which is often limited by time) doesn’t require a bigger donation from you, just a bit more time.

  • Organizing a small campaign like this is something you can also put on your resume, if you are applying for fundraising positions as a graphic designer, data analyst, event planner, prospect researcher, and more. There are ways to donate money without pulling directly from your wallet: for example, if you use Bing for a few search engine queries every month, you can donate the Microsoft Reward points to many causes: including UVU! When I worked at UVU, I had a job that required me to search online constantly for information, and I ended up raising $50+ for the University’s general fund. Not a huge amount, but I did not have to dip into my own finances for that particular donation.

  • There are other programs like this out there, and for a student on a budget (vs. a wealthy philanthropist) they are a simple way to contribute smaller one-time gifts to causes you want to support, but can’t justify using your own limited resources.

Macro-trends in Charitable Giving

  • Small-dollar donor participation is in steep decline
  • Similarly, total number of donors is going down
  • Institutional trust frequently shredded by scandal
  • lower total donors = small group of high-dollar donors everyone is chasing
  • Your average donor who isn’t a billionaire or multi-millionaire may be more compelled by one-off projects, or local programs, or things that they think have social impact that aren’t explicitly philanthropy

How it looks at UVU

  • Division is called Institutional Advancement
  • Campaigns are busy times at work
  • UVU set a goal of $350 million (they hit $250M)
  • advancement being the fancy higher ed word for fundraising
  • UVU does a couple odd things with how their org chart has shaken out in its details, but generally you’ll see these roles / teams in other higher ed fundraising teams, and many similarities with Hospital fundraising teams, Nonprofits are highly varied, so they may diverge, but the bigger ones also tend to have similar org charts
  • VP is in the University President’s Cabinet, one of the lead fundraisers, alongside the President
  • AVP of Development is the leader of the frontline fundraisers, and is a fundraiser themselves, they lead the development team (at UVU they are called Directors of Development, but job titles vary, like Major Gift Officer), these folks tend to be more extroverted, often working with specific colleges especially alongside the Deans to fund programs, scholarships, etc…and you have several related teams like Annual Giving, Planned Giving, Alumni Relations (if there wasn’t so much event planning, I would personally love to work in this world more), and Prospect Dev (sometimes Prospect Dev is on the data team instead, people sometimes don’t know where it fits)
  • Senior Director of Foundation Finance and Compliance, runs the endowment, has to host auditors when they drop by (a stressful day), leads the accountants, tries to keep everybody realistic
  • AVP of Strategic Engagement, leads the Executive Events team, and the Marketing & Communications (Marcom is common jargon there) team: this is where you have your web designers, your graphic designers, content creators, and event planners.
  • All these leaders often have executive assistants

How it looks in the for-profit world

  • Many companies have foundations or social impact missions that require operational staff
  • You will make more money in the for-profit world (e.g. an account manager at a Fortune 500 company instead of a development officer at a college)

Your values vs. your job

  • Utah culture around “make the money, be a philanthropist later”
